How to Choose the Right Paper Stock for Every Project

GSM weights, matte vs. gloss vs. uncoated finishes, and cardstock vs. bond paper — a practical guide before you place your next print order.

Elena Marsh

Print Production Lead

Jul 8, 2026 7 min read 2 comments

Paper is not an afterthought — it is half of every print job's finished feel. The same design can look premium or forgettable depending on the stock it lands on. Before you fill out an order form, it helps to understand the three things that actually change how paper looks, feels and performs: weight (GSM), finish, and stock category.

Understanding GSM: Paper Weight Explained

GSM stands for grams per square meter — it measures paper weight, not thickness (though the two usually track together). The higher the GSM, the heavier and sturdier the sheet. Here's how the common ranges break down for print jobs we run every week:

  • 70–90 GSM — standard copy and bond paper. Ideal for everyday documents, letters and internal reports.
  • 100–150 GSM — presentation and flyer weight. Feels noticeably more substantial without being stiff.
  • 200–250 GSM — light cardstock. Common for postcards, menus and greeting cards.
  • 300–350 GSM — premium cardstock. The go-to weight for business cards, invitations and portfolio covers.

Matte vs. Gloss vs. Uncoated: Which Finish Fits Your Project

Finish is the surface coating applied to paper, and it changes both how ink sits on the sheet and how the final piece feels in hand.

Matte finish

A smooth, low-glare coating that softens colors slightly and is easy to write on. It's the safest all-purpose choice for business cards, brochures and reports read under office lighting.

Gloss finish

A reflective coating that makes colors pop and photos look sharp and saturated. Best for photo prints, flyers and marketing pieces where vibrancy matters more than glare resistance.

Uncoated finish

Raw, natural paper texture with no coating at all. It has a warm, tactile feel that reads as premium and is the standard for letterheads, resumes and invitations you want to feel "authentic" rather than glossy.

"Customers rarely ask for a paper stock by name — they ask for a feeling. Our job is translating 'make it feel premium' into the right GSM and finish before a single sheet goes through the press."

— Elena Marsh, Print Production Lead

Cardstock vs. Bond Paper: When to Use Each

These two categories get confused often, but the choice comes down to how the piece will be handled:

  • Bond paper (70–100 GSM) is designed for reading and writing — it takes ink and toner cleanly and folds without cracking. Use it for letters, reports, manuals and anything with multiple pages.
  • Cardstock (200 GSM+) is built to be held, mailed or displayed. It resists bending and holds sharp die-cut edges, which is why it's the default for cards, tags and signage.
  • A common mistake: choosing cardstock for a stapled multi-page booklet. It looks great on the cover but jams staples and folds unevenly on interior pages — mix stocks instead, cardstock cover with bond interior.

Quick Reference: Paper Picks by Project

Still unsure? Here's the shortlist our production team defaults to when a customer doesn't specify a stock:

  • Business cards — 300–350 GSM cardstock, matte or soft-touch finish.
  • Reports & manuals — 80–90 GSM bond, uncoated, with a 250 GSM cardstock cover.
  • Photo prints — glossy or lustre photo paper, 250 GSM+.
  • Invitations — 300 GSM uncoated cotton or cardstock for a tactile, premium finish.
